Uses

Toxic parts

All members of this genus contain furocoumarins, which increase skin sensitivity to sunlight and may cause dermatitis[1].

Edible uses

There are no edible uses listed for Angelica laxiflora.

Material uses

There are no material uses listed for Angelica laxiflora.

Medicinal uses(Warning!)

The root is analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antirheumatic, nervine, sedative and vasodilator[2]. It is used in the treatment of colds with fever, headache and muscle ache, rheumatic pain - especially in the lower part of the body[2].

Propagation

Seed - best sown in a cold frame as soon as it is ripe since the seed only has a short viability[3]. Seed can also be sown in the spring, though germination rates will be lower. It requires light for germination[3]. When large enough to handle, prick the seedlings out into individual pots and grow them on in a cold frame for their first winter, planting them out into their permanent positions in the spring. The seed can also be sow in situ as soon as it is ripe.

Cultivation

We have very little information on this species and do not know how hardy it will be in Britain, though judging by its native range it could succeed outdoors in parts of this country. It is possibly no more than a synonym for A. pubescens[4]. The following notes are based on the general needs of the genus.

Requires a deep moist fertile soil in dappled shade or full sun[3].

Plants are reliably perennial if they are prevented from setting seed[3].
